**Re: Support for moving forward with a vacant lot tax**

Dear Chair Maple and Members of the Law and Legislation Committee,

I write in support of moving Sacramento toward a vacant lot tax.

Sacramento has a real vacancy problem, and the City is right to strengthen monitoring and enforcement. My analysis of Sacramento County 311 data since 2020 shows more than 1.5 million service records overall, including 1,574 calls tied to boarded-up or abandoned conditions across 1,282 unique addresses. Those calls are not spread evenly across the city. They cluster in a smaller number of ZIP codes and corridors, including 95815, 95838, and 95814, and along streets such as Broadway, J Street, Freeport Boulevard, Del Paso Boulevard, Stockton Boulevard, and Florin Road. My review of the parcel data also shows that Sacramento has roughly 28,670 vacant or underused parcels worth about $5.1 billion in assessed land value. In other words, this is not a small or isolated issue.

But better enforcement alone will not solve the main problem. A complaint-driven system can respond to neglect after neighbors and businesses are already dealing with it, but it does not change the financial incentive to hold land vacant for years. My analysis of the 311 and parcel data shows that abandoned-property complaints are linked to both residential and commercial parcels, which means the costs of vacancy are felt by nearby residents, business corridors, and the public. When a property sits empty year after year, the harm is not abstract. It shows up as repeated complaints, visible blight, weaker commercial streets, and delayed reuse of land that could serve housing, jobs, or neighborhood needs. A very low flat fee does not change that behavior in a meaningful way.

Therefore, Sacramento should treat enhanced enforcement as a foundation, not the final answer. A vacant lot tax is the tool that can actually change the math of long-term speculation and make activation, sale, or redevelopment the better choice. I urge the Committee to support a policy path that includes both stronger monitoring now and a future tax structure that is scaled enough to matter. I also urge the City to collect the data needed to make that work well, including parcel size, use type, ownership, and length of vacancy. If the City wants fewer long-term vacant properties, the policy must do more than document vacancy. It must make keeping land idle more expensive than putting it to use.

Thank you for your consideration.

Respectfully,  
Scott Coffin
